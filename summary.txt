### Section 2.1: Atoms, Isotopes, Ions, and Molecules
Life is fundamentally composed of **matter** (anything that occupies space and has mass). Of the 118 known elements, 98 occur naturally, with **oxygen (O), carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and nitrogen (N)** being the four most abundant in living organisms. 

* **Atomic Structure:** An atom is the smallest unit of an element that retains its chemical properties. It consists of a dense central **nucleus** containing positively charged **protons** and uncharged **neutrons** (each weighing approximately 1 atomic mass unit or Dalton), surrounded by negatively charged **electrons** of negligible mass orbiting in shells.
* **Atomic Number and Mass:** The number of protons determines an element’s atomic number. The sum of protons and neutrons determines its mass number. 
* **Isotopes:** Isotopes are forms of an element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (e.g., Carbon-12 vs. Carbon-14). Unstable isotopes, called **radioisotopes**, lose energy through radioactive decay. Because decay occurs at a predictable rate (measured in **half-lives**), scientists use them for radiometric dating of fossils.
* **Electron Configuration & Bonding:** Based on the Bohr model, electrons fill energy levels (shells) sequentially. The **octet rule** dictates that atoms are most stable when their outermost (valence) shell contains eight electrons (or two for the innermost shell). Modern quantum mechanics describes these regions of high probability as **orbitals** ($s, p, d, f$).
* **Types of Bonds:** 
  * **Ionic Bonds:** Formed when atoms transfer electrons to achieve stability, creating positively charged **cations** and negatively charged **anions** that attract each other.
  * **Covalent Bonds:** Formed when atoms share electrons. These are the most common and strongest bonds in biological systems. They can be **nonpolar** (equal sharing) or **polar** (unequal sharing due to differences in **electronegativity**, which creates partial positive and negative charges).
  * **Weak Bonds:** **Hydrogen bonds** form from the attraction between a partially positive hydrogen atom and an electronegative atom (like oxygen or nitrogen). **Van der Waals interactions** are weak, temporary attractions caused by fluctuating electron densities.

### Section 2.2: Water
Water ($H_2O$) is a highly polar molecule because oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen. This polarity allows water molecules to form **hydrogen bonds** with one another and with other substances, giving rise to several unique properties essential for life:

* **Hydrophilic vs. Hydrophobic:** Polar substances that dissolve easily in water are hydrophilic ("water-loving"), while nonpolar substances like fats and oils are hydrophobic ("water-fearing").
* **Density of Ice:** Unlike most liquids, water becomes *less* dense as a solid. As water freezes, hydrogen bonds lock the molecules into a spacious crystalline lattice, allowing ice to float and insulate aquatic life underneath.
* **High Heat Capacity and Vaporization:** Water has a high specific heat capacity, meaning it absorbs and releases large amounts of heat with minimal temperature change. This stabilizes climates and internal body temperatures. Its high heat of vaporization allows organisms to cool themselves through evaporation (e.g., sweating).
* **Solvent Properties:** Water acts as an excellent solvent. It disrupts ionic bonds in compounds like salt through **dissociation**, surrounding individual ions in **spheres of hydration**.
* **Cohesion and Adhesion:** Water molecules stick to each other (**cohesion**), creating high surface tension. They also stick to other polar surfaces (**adhesion**). Together, these forces enable **capillary action**, allowing plants to transport water from roots to leaves.
* **pH and Buffers:** Water naturally dissociates into hydrogen ($H^+$) and hydroxide ($OH^-$) ions. The **pH scale** (0 to 14) measures $H^+$ concentration; values below 7 are acidic, while values above 7 are alkaline (basic). To maintain homeostasis, living systems utilize **buffers** (such as the carbonic acid-bicarbonate system in human blood) to absorb excess $H^+$ or $OH^-$ and prevent dangerous pH fluctuations.

### Section 2.3: Carbon
Carbon is the fundamental structural "backbone" of all organic macromolecules (proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids) because it has **four valence electrons**, allowing it to form up to four covalent bonds.

* **Hydrocarbons:** Organic molecules containing only carbon and hydrogen (like methane) store large amounts of energy in their covalent bonds. Hydrocarbons can form linear chains (**aliphatic**) or closed rings (**aromatic**). 
* **Isomers:** Isomers are molecules with the same chemical formula but different structural arrangements, which alters their physical and chemical properties:
  * **Structural Isomers:** Differ in the covalent placement of their bonds (e.g., butane and isobutane).
  * **Geometric Isomers:** Differ in the spatial arrangement around double bonds, leading to *cis* (bent backbone) or *trans* (linear backbone, as in trans fats) configurations.
  * **Enantiomers:** Mirror-image, non-superimposable structures (such as D- and L-amino acids). Only specific mirror images are active in biological systems.
* **Functional Groups:** Specific groups of atoms (such as hydroxyl, carboxyl, amino, methyl, phosphate, and sulfhydryl) attach to carbon backbones, determining the macromolecule's chemical behavior, acidity/basicity, and whether it is hydrophilic or hydrophobic.
* **The Carbon Cycle:** Carbon constantly cycles through the atmosphere, biosphere, and oceans. Human activities, particularly deforestation and the burning of fossil fuels, disrupt this cycle, causing atmospheric $CO_2$ levels to rise and oceans to acidify.
